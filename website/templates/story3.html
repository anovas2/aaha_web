Need, Subsidies, and Reality, 2014-2018
Need vs. Actual with Negative Capacity
  • This visualization shows the need for housing assistance on the top half and plots it against the available supply to show the deficit number of housing assistance in any form that is available to assist those in need.
  • The number of subsidized housing units in the city of Atlanta is well below what is needed for those that are rent burdened as well as low income.
  • Using Tableau’s forecasting model (set at a 90% confidence interval) the housing need is projected at 5K units over the next four years with 17 active subsidies.
  • There is debate in Affordable Housing Planning between locating projects with the goal to improve neighborhoods as a community development project and with the goal to improve the living situation of participating low-income households through access to resources.
  • One largely argued opinion is that Atlanta Housing should devote more funding to rental subsidies that are affixed to specific rental properties. Potentially one that would focus on the low income population.
Renters and rentals across Metro ATL, 2014-2018
Subisidies by Number and Type Over Time
  • In the mid-1990’s Atlanta adopted a new approach to public housing. One that was designed to help lift people and neighborhoods out of “isolated blight and poverty.” The new design was to be a solution for both low-income residents and economic development in the city. The was all positioned under the HOPE VI program.
  • The HOPE VI program also changed the demographics of the neighborhoods, by replacing 100% publich housing units with only 40% public housing units, 23% rent subsidized, and 36% market rate. Instead of constructing, managing and maintaining public housing as a form of direct governance. Housing authorities incentivized private developers to create market-quality housing with an affordability component.
  • The most popular way that public & private partnershiops fostered affordable housing development was through Low Income Housing Tax Credits. LIHTC’s are the largest supplier of affordable and that has declined dramatically over the last several years.
  • The Great Recession effectively stalled the housing authority efforts. By 2012 the supply of affordable housing in Atlanta droped 25%. Agency leaders pushed more and more private landlords to accept authority-backed Section 8 vouchers. Currently, the only viable and meaningful housing assistance in now Housing Choice Vouchers, aka, section 8 housing assistance.
Renters and rentals across Metro ATL, 2014-2018
Subsidies Available vs. Usable
  • We’ve shown you the number of people who need housing assistance. We’ve shown you the supply of the housing assistance available via the various programs. The most widely available and widely used is the Housing Choice Voucher, often called Section 8 housing assistance. We wanted to deepen your awareness of how this program really works. The median wait time to receive HCV assistance is 1.5 years but can be in excess of 7 years in some markets. Once you have received a HCV, a recipient has 60 days to find suitable housing or they lose it. The burden is squarely on the HCV recipient. On average, they must research 39 posted housing choices before finding one that will consider accepting the HCV. Of these 1 in 39 properties, the acceptance rate is only 24.5% and over half of these possible acceptances (55%) have conditions specified that have to be met. Additionally the bulk of these acceptances are in high poverty areas with poor schools, no public transportation, and minimal services. Hardly where people want to live. The denial rate is much higher in low poverty areas.
  • Challenges for the property owners include lengthy and expensive inspections to be able to rent the property to the HCV recipient. The HCV amount may also be significantly under the fair market rent for the unit as HUD has few resources to stay current on market rents and lacks funding to increase the HCV amounts to match the FMR.
